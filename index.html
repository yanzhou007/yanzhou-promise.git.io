Language, interaction, and culture

I'm a Ph.D. candidate in the Department of Asian Languages and Cultures at University of California, Los Angeles.  I'm a linguist who approaches human interaction as a collaboration of multimodal resources, including grammar, prosody, gestures, and other visual behaviors.

My current research focuses on when and how people accomplish different social actions, esp. promise-making in institutional and everyday conversations. At UCLA, I primarily work with Hongyin Tao, Iwasaki Shoichi, Sung-Ock Sohn, and Steven Clayman. I'm a member of Center for Language, Interaction, and Culture (CLIC) and Conversation Analysis Working Group (CAWG) at UCLA. 

I am also interested in second language pedagogy and teacher-student interaction in second language classrooms. I am experienced in teaching all levels of Mandarin classes and content courses in Chinese language and culture, as well as second language pedagogy. 

Outside of academia, I enjoy coffee, cooking, hiking, and street photography.

Introduction to Chinese linguistics

This is an introductory course of Chinese linguistics. Students learn the sound system, writing system, and its reform, regional differences, major structural features of Chinese. Students also explore Chinese languages in society and in cultural practices.
​
I have taught this course in summer sessions both as an instructor and a teaching assistant.

2

Topics in Chinese Language and Culture

This course explores the inter-dependent relationship between the Chinese language and culture.  Students learn to use basic concepts in socio-cultural linguistics, Discourse Analysis, as well as technology to analyze the Chinese language and the cultural conventions expressed through verbal and non-verbal linguistic devices. Major coverage is on language use as reflected in everyday interaction as well as in various types of media: film, television, internet, advertisement, and so forth.

Instructor: Prof. Hongyin Tao

3

Asian Language Pedagogy

This course investigates major issues in teaching and learning of Asian languages. Students learn pedagogical grammar, curricular development, social, cultural, and cognitive foundations of Asian language acquisition, best practices in teaching Asian language writing systems, special issues in teaching heritage students, comparisons of K-12 language teaching and college language teaching, assessment methods, and emerging trends in Asian language teaching. Students acquire practical teaching skills through classroom observation, lesson planning, and mircroteaching.

Instructor: Prof. Sung-Ock S. Sohn

RESEARCH PROJECTS

Promise-making in institutional and everyday conversation

My dissertation, Promising and Commitment to Future Actions in Mandarin Conversation, studies when people make promises in everyday interaction and how they design their promises using multimodal resources.
For instance, what kind of promises are considered "big" promises? What are the linguistic or non-linguistic features of these "big" promises, and how "big" should your promises be? My research shows that the more severe the consequence of the promised action is, the "bigger" promise people make. Check out my paper:
The principle of proportionality: Consequentiality and promises in Mandarin conversation. Chinese language and discourse 11(1):107-133.

As a part of my graduate certificate in Digital Humanities program at UCLA, I started my first DH project on promises made by American politicians in Professor Miriam Posner's DH201 class, and will expand this project by comparing politicians' promises from the U.S. and China. 

Epistemic stance in Mandarin

You might have noticed that people say "maybe" a lot! Epistemic stance refers to the degree of speakers commitment (how certain) to the poroposition uttered in the speech. Many studies have shown that speakers tend to downgrade their certainty in conversations in order to follow the politeness norms (Broan and Levinson, 1987). My research examines social actions accomplished in those situations. This study also finds that Chinese speakers seems to "pretend" to be uncertain even more often than English speakers! Check out my paper :
What are speakers doing when they pretend to be uncertain?: Actions with non-committal epistemic stance in Mandarin Conversation?

Inclusivity in Chinese as a second language classroom

An inclusive classroom is a safe and supportive learning community that recognizes and respects every member's values, identities, and cultures. It requires teachers and students to stand against any forms of explicit or implicit biases of race, ethnicity, religion, gender, sexual orientation, disability, gender identity, immigration status, or any other protected classification.
Research also shows that groups with higher social diversity share more information and perform better than those with lower social diversity.
In the current Chinese as a second language (CSL) field, however, teaching materials and pedagogical practices relevant to inclusive classrooms have not been extensively discussed yet. To call for teachers and researchers' attention and to promote inclusive teaching in CSL field, I am collaborating with colleagues at UCLA on the Inclusive teaching in CSL Classroom project, including a special issue currently under review in the Journal of Technology and Chinese Language Teaching, an open-access website with resources for researchers and instructors (supported by the Mellon-EPIC innovation Grant at UCLA).
