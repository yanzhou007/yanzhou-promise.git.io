About this project


Politicians' promises is a Digital Humanities project developed in the course DH201 in 2019 at UCLA. It's also a side project of Yan's dissertation Promises and commitment to future actions. Contact Yan or visit her academic website to learn more about the project.

BACKGROUND

Speech Act Theory

 “By saying something, we do something.” ----- Austin (1962) 

Human societies and communities may be organized in various forms, but one thing we share is the privilege of language and interaction. For a long time, linguistic studies have been focused on the structure of languages (phonetics, phonology, syntax, and semantics), but human languages are more than that. In 1960s, philosopher Austin firstly proposed the idea of Speech Act; John Searle (yes, that Berkley philosophy professor being accused of sexual assault, but we’ll focus on his academic contribution here for today) then continued to develop this theory and firstly analyzed promising as a social act (Searle 1969). Searle defines promising as a subtype of commissive speech acts and is accomplished by a proposition that expresses speaker’s intention to do something in the future that the hearer wishes. A set of conditions and principles must be fulfilled to practice a sincere promise, such as the point or purpose of the act, the relative positions of the speaker and the hearer, the degree of commitment undertaken.

Politicians’ promises might be different (dramatic?) from ordinary people’s but should follow some basic linguistic formula of promises, such as lexical verbs and nouns of commitment (e.g. promise, commit, vow, etc.), modal verbs (e.g. must, should, will, etc.) and semi-modals (e.g. be going to, want to, need to, etc.). This project tries to explore the usage of these linguistic devices in politicians’ promises in important speeches. The current version focuses on two main questions: (1) Whether presidents’ preferences of promise-expressions have changed throughout U.S. history; (2) Do governors across the country have similar preferences of promise expressions?

Modals and modality system

Modality is a grammatical category that marks ability, necessity, permissions and obligation in utterances. The categorization of modality varies, this project follows Palmer’s (2001) modal system, which includes two main categories--propositional and event--and four subcategories--epistemic and evidential, as well as deontic and dynamic. Epistemic modality indicates speakers’ stance on the factual status of the proposition, often marked by modal verbs in English such as maybe, might, must; evidential modality indicates speaker’s evidence for the factual status, marked by expressions such as it is said, and I heard; deontic modality expresses obligations or permissions from external sources, marked by modal verbs such as must and should, and semi-modal have to, ought to; dynamic modality indicates speakers willingness or ability that comes from individuals internal concerns, marked by modal verbs such as will, can, and shall.

Among these modalities, most deontic and dynamic modals are relevant to promises and commitment in interaction, some epistemic modals are also used in promises. In Modern English, it is recognized that there are nine central modal verbs, including can, could, may, might, must, shall, should, will, would. In addition, some words known as semi-modals also function as modals in interaction, such as have to, want to, ought to, need to (Biber et al. 1999, 73,483).

Based on the categorizations by Palmer (2001) and Biber et al. (1999), these common promise-relevant modals are grouped into three types: volition (willingness), obligation, and ability/possibility (epistemic modals may and might, as well as would are less relevant to promises and are found no occurrence of commitment-expressing cases in the current dataset.) These modals, along with lexical verbs and nouns expressing commitment (such as commitment, assure, promise, etc.), will be searched as promise-expressions in the database. The frequency results will be analyzed and visualized to answer the two main questions in this project.

DATASET

The current database includes U.S. presidents' inaugural speeches and the State of the States Addresses (governors' new year speeches) in 2019. The governors' speech data are downloaded from the  What America’s Governors Are Talking About project (see Github) by the group FiveThirtyEight. Inaugural speeches are downloaded from The American Presidency Project hosted by UCSB. 

METHODOLOGY

Corpus linguistic programs Lancsbox developed by Lancaster University is used to analyze the normalized frequencies of the promise-expressions and to generate the concordances. Since many modals can express multiple modalities (e.g. must as a epistemic/possibility modal and a deontic/obligation modal), annotations to distinguish these functions are yet to be added. 

The concordance lines extracted are then further analyzed with TopicModelingTool. Note this is not the main focus on this project and the topic modeling process is very preliminary. However, the current result might provide us a glimpse of the content of promises made by different presidents and governors.

VISUALIZATION

Visualization is realized through Fourish. Frequencies of different expressions are visualized with Grif of column charts, stacked area charts, stacked bar charts, map. For governors' promise section, an interactive map of U.S. is created to show main topics of promises made by governors across the country and the frequencies of promise-expressions identified in the current project. The current version of the website only show pictures of visualizations with external links to the Flourish pages.
